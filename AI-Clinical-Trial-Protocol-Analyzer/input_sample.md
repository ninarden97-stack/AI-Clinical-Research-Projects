## 2. BACKGROUND & RATIONALE
2.1 Overview
Human fatty acid synthase (FASN) is the sole cytosolic enzyme responsible for de novo synthesis of palmitate (1-3). It has seven catalytic domains (β-ketoacyl synthase, malonyl/acetyltransferase, dehydrogenase, enoyl reductase, β-ketoacyl reductase, acyl carrier protein, and thioesterase) and synthesizes palmitate by condensing acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA (1). Breast cancers with high levels of FASN are much more likely to recur and metastasize with significantly shorter disease free and overall survival (2-5). FASN induces resistance to multiple anticancer drugs including doxorubicin and cisplatin (6-8).
The typical Western diet contains sufficient free fatty acids so that FASN is not required for normal cell function and, thus, its expression is very low in normal cells except lactating breast, cycling endometrium, and fatty tissues. In contrast, breast cancer cells require de novo fatty acid synthesis for survival. Inhibition of FASN induces apoptosis selectively in cancer cells both in vitro and in vivo (9-12) with minimal effect on normal cells (6, 13, 14). Thus, FASN drives breast cancer survival and is an ideal therapeutic target. In this application, we will target FASN to stop breast cancer growth.
Having identified FASN as an important therapeutic target, we began searching for an inhibitor. In preliminary studies, we found that the proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) effectively inhibit FASN, reduce breast cancer cell survival, and restore sensitivity to chemotherapy (15). PPIs are FDA-approved for treatment of a variety of acid-related diseases that plague the digestive system, are widely used, and are well tolerated without major adverse effects (16-18). Interestingly, a recent retrospective study in head and neck cancer patients reported an increased overall survival associated with PPI use (19). Chinese investigators reported intriguing results of a phase II trial of intermittent high dose esomeprazole in combination with docetaxel and cisplatin in patients with metastatic breast cancer (NCT01069081, 20). Median PFS was 7.5 months in the control group compared to 9.5 months in PPI treated patients (p=0.030); impact as greatest in the subset of patients with triple negative disease (n=17, 3.3 vs. 9.5 months, p=0.014). Though small, the results of this trial are consistent with our hypothesis and support further exploration. Thus, we hypothesize that the PPIs may be repositioned as safe and effective drugs to augment the effect of chemotherapy without adding significantly to the cost or toxicity of therapy.

2.2 The Role of FASN Function in Drug Resistance
FASN up-regulation in doxorubicin-resistant breast cancer cells. While comparing protein profiles between the parental MCF7 and its doxorubicin-selected and resistant derivative MCF7/AdVp3000 (M3K) cells, we found a 270-kDa protein with increased expression in the drug resistant M3K cells. This protein was identified to be human FASN by MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry. Functional analysis showed that FASN activity was dramatically (5 fold) up-regulated in M3K cells. FASN up-regulation in M3K cells was validated using Western blot analysis. During the stepwise selection of M3K cells, cell lines (e.g., M10 and M100) with low and intermediate doxorubicin resistance levels were also generated. Interestingly, these cells also showed increased FASN level but to a less extent than M3K cells.
FASN over-expression causes resistance to multiple DNA-damaging agents. To investigate whether FASN up-regulation contributes to drug resistance in M3K cells, we first performed stable FASN knockdown using shRNA and tested cellular response to doxorubicin and mitoxantrone. FASN knockdown significantly reduced resistance of M3K cells to both doxorubicin and mitoxantrone. We next performed the reverse experiment to over-express ectopic FASN in the parental MCF7 cells. A stable MCF7 clone with FASN over-expression (FASN) was generated, and as predicted, it was significantly more resistant to doxorubicin and mitoxantrone than the control Vec clone. The FASN-mediated doxorubicin resistance was also dramatically reduced using the known FASN inhibitor, orlistat. FASN over-expression also significantly increased resistance to etopside, cisplatin, and ionizing radiation (IR) but had no effect on cellular response to paclitaxel and vinblastine.
To ensure that the impact of FASN was not unique to MCF-7 cells, we performed FASN knockdown and survival analysis of another breast cancer cell line, MDA-MB-468. FASN knockdown is accompanied by significant reduction in cellular resistance to both doxorubicin and mitoxantrone. Similar results were observed with MDA-MB-231 cells.
FASN silencing does not increase chemo-sensitivity in non-malignant cells. We hypothesized that non-malignant cells would be less dependent on FASN expression, and thus would not be affected by FASN inhibition. FASN knockdown had no significant effect on cellular response to doxorubicin or mitoxantrone in MCF10A1 cells.

2.3 Targeting FASN to Reverse Chemotherapy Resistance
Identification of PPIs as FASN TE inhibitors. To identify FDA-approved drugs that target FASN TE, we performed in-silico screening of a library of 2,417 FDA-approved drugs using DOCK programs and the crystal structure of FASN TE (ID: 3TJM). The 200 top-scoring compounds were clustered based on chemical structure and 25 representative drugs were tested using the 4-MUH fluorogenic assay. Pantoprazole dose-dependently inhibited FASN TE activity with a Ki of 4.1 μM. Other PPIs (omeprazole, lansoprazole, rabeprazole) showed similar inhibition with Ki values of 3.4–5.9 μM.
Molecular dynamics simulations showed favorable binding free energy for all PPIs, with omeprazole having the strongest predicted binding. Structural modeling suggests PPIs interact with the catalytic triad and specificity channel of FASN TE, providing theoretical support for inhibition.
PPIs inhibit and bind to cellular FASN. In MCF7 cell lysates, 10 μM PPIs reduced >75% FASN activity and 50 μM reduced >95%. Lansoprazole inhibited FP-probe labeling of FASN in a dose-dependent manner, indicating selective binding to FASN TE.
PPIs inhibit breast cancer cell proliferation and sensitize resistant cells. All PPIs inhibited proliferation of MDA-MB-231 cells with IC50 values of 5.6–12.2 μM. Omeprazole was most effective. PPIs induced apoptosis in a dose-dependent manner. Lansoprazole reduced doxorubicin IC50 in resistant M3K cells and showed synergistic effects in combination assays.

2.4 Pharmaco‑epidemiologic Support for Use of PPIs
A retrospective analysis of 6,323 breast cancer patients from the Indiana Network for Patient Care (INPC) showed significantly increased overall survival in patients who took PPIs after diagnosis. Among 488 TNBC patients, PPI use also showed a protective effect. Analyses were adjusted for age, tumor stage, and time of first diagnosis.

2.5 Neoadjuvant Therapy in Breast Cancer
Neoadjuvant chemotherapy improves breast conservation and allows assessment of in vivo sensitivity. Only ~35% of TNBC patients achieve pCR. pCR is associated with significantly improved survival.
This study proposes a single-arm phase II trial of omeprazole + standard neoadjuvant chemotherapy to generate safety, PK, biologic, and preliminary efficacy data needed for a future randomized trial.

## 3. OBJECTIVES
3.1 PRIMARY OBJECTIVE
Estimate the rate of pCR in patients with triple negative breast cancer and FASN expression treated with standard neoadjuvant chemotherapy in combination with high dose omeprazole.

3.2 SECONDARY OBJECTIVES
• Quantify the number of patients with newly diagnosed TNBC with tumors that express FASN.
• Estimate the rate of pCR in patients with triple negative breast cancer (irrespective of FASN status) treated with standard neoadjuvant chemotherapy in combination with high dose omeprazole.
• Describe the safety of incorporating high dose omeprazole with standard neoadjuvant chemotherapy.
• Estimate the biologic activity of high dose omeprazole in modulating FASN expression and activity.

3.3 TERTIARY / EXPLORATORY / CORRELATIVE OBJECTIVES
• Describe omeprazole exposure.
• Compare peak omeprazole concentration to level associated with activity based on preclinical testing (~5.6 μM based on IC50).
• Using PK‑PD models, use omeprazole concentration and change in lipid levels to predict clinical outcome.
• Explore changes in lipid profile over time.

3.4 PRIMARY OUTCOME MEASURE
pCR is defined as no invasive disease in the breast or axilla at the time of definitive surgery.

3.5 SECONDARY OUTCOME MEASURES
• Number of patients expressing FASN in tumor.
• pCR rate in all patients.
• Safety based on NCI CTC Version 4.0.
• Change in FASN expression from baseline to after 4–7 days on omeprazole and at surgery.
• Change in FASN activity from baseline to after 4–7 days on omeprazole and at surgery.
• Change in downstream target gene expression (e.g., PARP1 and SP1).

3.6 TERTIARY / EXPLORATORY / CORRELATIVE OUTCOME MEASURES
• Omeprazole exposure (PK).
• Comparison of peak omeprazole concentration to preclinical activity levels.
• PK‑PD modeling of omeprazole exposure, FASN activity, lipid levels, and pCR.
• Changes in lipid levels from baseline to after 4–7 days on omeprazole and at surgery.

10.3.1 Special Notes on Assessment of Progression of Non‑Target Disease
10.3.1.1 When the patient also has measurable disease
To declare unequivocal progression based solely on non‑target lesions:
There must be substantial worsening of non‑target disease
The worsening must be significant enough that overall tumor burden has clearly increased, even if target lesions show SD (Stable Disease) or PR (Partial Response)
A modest increase in non‑target lesions is not enough
Declaring PD based only on non‑target disease in the presence of PR/SD in target lesions is extremely rare

10.3.1.2 When the patient has only non‑measurable disease
This occurs in trials where measurable disease is not required for entry.

Key principles:
Non‑measurable disease cannot be quantified precisely
To declare unequivocal progression, the increase in disease burden must be substantial

A useful rule:
The worsening should be comparable to what would qualify as PD in measurable disease
→ i.e., equivalent to ~73% increase in tumor volume, which corresponds to a 20% increase in diameter

Examples of unequivocal progression:
Pleural effusion increasing from “trace” → “large”
Lymphangitic spread increasing from localized → widespread
Worsening described as “sufficient to require a change in therapy”
If unequivocal progression is seen → patient is considered PD at that time.

Omeprazole is a substituted benzimidazole that:
Inhibits the H+/K+ ATPase (gastric proton pump)
Blocks the final step of gastric acid production
Inhibits both basal and stimulated acid secretion
Accumulates in gastric mucosa for ≥1 day after plasma clearance

16.1 DEFINITIONS
16.1.1 Adverse Event (AE)
An AE is:
Any unplanned, unwanted event
Possibly or not related to study therapy
Includes new conditions or worsening of pre‑existing ones
Identified via exam, labs, symptoms
AEs are monitored until:
Resolved
Determined to be due to chronic/stable conditions
Or due to intercurrent illness

16.1.2 Suspected Adverse Reaction (SAR)
A SAR is:
An AE with a reasonable possibility of being caused by the drug
Evidence may include:
Single rare event strongly associated with drug exposure
Uncommon events occurring more frequently in treated patients
Aggregate analyses showing higher incidence in treatment arm

16.1.3 Unanticipated Problem (UP)
Must meet all three:
Unexpected in nature/severity/frequency
Related or possibly related to research
Suggests increased risk to subjects or others
UPs may involve:
Social or economic harm
Increased risk without actual harm

16.1.4 Adverse Reaction (AR)
An AE caused by the drug.

16.1.4 Serious Adverse Event (SAE)
SAE includes:
Death
Life‑threatening event
Hospitalization ≥24 hours or prolongation
Persistent disability
Congenital anomaly
Important medical events requiring intervention
Pregnancy (participant or partner)
Pregnancy management:
Female participant → discontinue study drug
Partner of male participant → male may continue
Pregnancy followed to outcome

16.1.5 Unexpected Adverse Event
An AE whose nature/severity is not listed in protocol, IB, product insert, or consent.

16.1.6 Attribution Categories
Unrelated
Unlikely
Possible
Probable
Definite

16.1.7 Classification & Grading
Uses CTCAE v4.0.
